Why most freelancers undercharge — and how to fix it

Setting your hourly rate is one of the most consequential financial decisions you'll make. Most freelancers pick a number based on what competitors charge or what "feels right" — both methods consistently produce rates that are too low.

The correct approach is to work backwards from what you need: your desired net income, plus the taxes and expenses that come out of every euro you earn, divided by the real number of hours you can actually bill per year. That's the floor. Everything above it is profit.

A common mistake: counting all working hours as billable. Realistically, 30–40% of your time goes to admin, sales, client communication, and onboarding — none of it billed. Build this into your calculation.

How to use this calculator

Desired monthly net income — what you want to actually take home after everything. Not gross revenue.

Monthly expenses — software subscriptions, home office, phone, courses. These come out before tax in most countries.

Billable hours/week — be honest. If you work 40 hours but only 25 are billable, use 25.

Weeks worked/year — deduct holidays, sick days, and quiet periods. 46 weeks is a reasonable baseline.

The 20% profit buffer gives you room for slow months, reinvestment, and savings. Never quote at your bare floor.

Frequently asked questions

How many billable hours per week is realistic?

Most independent freelancers bill 20–30 hours per week consistently. If you're regularly hitting 35+, you're likely undercharging — because your rate is cheap enough that you need to stay constantly busy to make ends meet.

Should I charge all clients the same rate?

No. Many experienced freelancers use a tiered approach: lower rates for long-term retainers (less uncertainty), higher rates for short-notice or one-off projects. Your rate should reflect both the work and the relationship.

When should I raise my rates?

At minimum, annually — to keep pace with inflation. Tactically: raise rates when you're fully booked, when your skills have visibly improved, or when a client has been with you more than 2 years without a review. A 10–20% annual increase is normal for established freelancers.

How do I tell a client my rate went up?

Give 30 days notice, be direct, and frame it as a review rather than an apology. "My rate from [date] will be €X/hr." No negotiation, no excessive explanation. Clients who respect your work will accept it. Those who don't were never your ideal clients.
